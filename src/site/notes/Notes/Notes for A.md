# What was the span of time you were playing as a musician apart of bands (till u moved here)

My first proper gig was 1992. I moved to Melbourne at the start of 2006. So 1992 to 2005.
# How many bands did you play with (before moving to Melbourne?
As a horn player I was a session musician and freelancer, so the honest answer is more than I could remember. For my own sake I spent a couple of minutes doing a rough tally:
- My own jazz ensembles, including my quartet, quintet, sextet.
- Many jazz ensembles as sideman / member including 3 big bands or orchestras I can think of. Played Sydney Olympics, Manly Jazz Festival, a few recordings
- Open Source Project & Tsunami - co-led experimental jazz / noise projects. Played perth festival, toured to Melbourne
- bands that were not jazz/experimental: 
	- 5 or 6 original rock / alt bands, including prawns with horns, 3 with label deal releases, 3 aus tours and 2 europe tours with prawns.
	- 3 funk bands 
	- 5 latin (salsa/cuban) and african bands
	- a few cover bands (popular ones for money, plus a few jazz tribute projects)

# Did u just play saxophone?
I started on piano as a kid. At primary school I had to choose between trumpet and clarinet; I picked clarinet. In high school the clarinet was an entry drug to the saxophone (both are reed instruments). I play some instruments well and others less so, in the latter category I taught myself electric bass in school and played a lot of drums throughout university and afterwards. Drums is really great for non-drummers to practice, especially with a limited kit or even just a snare: it removes harmony and scale so you can focus on rhythmic aspects, and learn how even a single drum can be melodic.

# What drew u to music?
Like most kids I was into music. My school was a big sport school and the band program was a cool escape. The jazz big band was pretty good and high status, with some tours and plenty of gigs. Some older students had a jazz combo that everyone idolised. The band thing was really just the coolest option available.

As I got older there was a bunch of really good musicians at my school at the same time. I continued to work with a few of them through my career, and one is widely acclaimed and famous in jazz circles. I ended up auditioning and getting into the WA youth jazz orchestra (WAYJO), which was a pipeline into the Conservatorium.

# What kept you there ?
The jazz scene in Perth at the time was going through a golden age. WAYJO was stacked and the scene was very tight - it was a few hundred  people from all  walks of life that all shared a comraderie and bond. Once I got into the Conservatorium there was jam sessions all the time, gigs to go to or play at almost every night. I'd go to a gig on any given tuesday and hang out with 10 or 50 people that were friends, and go to house party jam sessions afterwards. This type of close community is kind of intoxicating in a way.

Music is a big, broad scene with lots of subcultures, and where the good musicians have respect for other subcultures. I could go to blues, or punk, or electronic gigs and sit in the band, hang out, make new friends and opportunities. While the majority of jazz students were somewhat 'conservative' in being focused on the usual types of jazz, there was a good amount who had diverse interests. So I got exposed to music that blew my mind as much as jazz. Classical music was a big thing for me, especially Messiaen, Ravel, Stravinsky, and others.  Some stuff was jazz adjacent like NY downtown music (Lounge Lizards, John Zorn) which was closely related to Mr Bungle. Zorn and Bungle became huge influences on my music. Other music I was introduced to was the IDM of the time, especially Aphex Twin and Squarepusher. All of these led me to working with musicians in those genres, for example I played a duo show for the Perth Festival with an electronic music producer from Germany in the early 2000s.

# What took you away?
I was increasingly interested in the cutting edge, new music, especially experimental stuff. While in the late 1990s I was taking lessons in NY and focused on 'hard bop', by the early 2000s I was moving into original projects influenced by Zorn, Bungle, and others. I had also been working more with groups in other genres. This put a little distance between me and my OG jazz friends, many of which were now moving to NYC to develop their careers. I also began touring more with my experimental stuff and with the prawns with horns, which impacted my ability to hold down regular positions in jazz bands in perth. 
At the same time I was getting sick of teaching at high schools. Most of my students were dialling it in, not practicing, it was an uninspired experience. I ended up taking a six-month contract for a cruise ship gig... the other musicians were good friends from the jazz scene, and other entertainers (dancers, actors) were also friends from WAAPA. 
The contract went pear shaped quickly. We all flew to where the ship was based (in Singapore) to prepare and rehearse. The ship ended up stuck in port due to contract disputes between the port and owners. After a month of hanging out in Singapore waiting for instructions we were all flown back to Perth to begin cruises albeit on a different ship. This causes flow on issues: the employer ended up going under a month and a half into the six month contract. I'd quit all my regular gigs and teaching. 

A year later we moved to Melbourne. I had lots of connections and friends here but I was also moving to a new scene without established gigs and not at the top of people's minds. Not long after I had my first child (😉), and decided that the gigging life being out at night was not the lifestyle I wanted with a young child. I wanted to be around, and enjoy that time rather than be the stereotypically absent musician father.  

# What was your earliest memory with music that made u see it as something u wanted to pursue?

A turning point when I was in my early teens was getting given cassette tapes by two uncles at around the same time. It was all 60s music. One uncle gave me a tape with Hendrix on one side and Led Zeppelin on the other. The other uncle's tape had the Coltrane Quartet on one side - [My Favourite Things](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ehYM_cg2DHI) - and that music is really the same music as Hendrix. I think the Jimi Hendrix Experience was quite influenced by Coltrane. It absolutely captured me. I realised the saxophone could also make amazing music, not just cheesy pop or band songs. I started practicing pretty hard - up to 8 hours a day on weekends - and learning from Jamie Oehlers, who had just graduated from Berklee and later won a major international competition. 
# Do u have any regrets about the choices made during or after u were playing?
I don't have any regrets as such: the things we do change us, lead us to grow in ways we can't calculate, and I carry my life experiences into each moment. Music gave me a lot and my life was already flowing in different directions. I'm a big advocate of following the flow of life rather than forcing things; its not always a great life recipe for great wealth, but great wealth is rarely a recipe for a great life. 

Having said that, there were a few decisions that I knew at the time I was doing due to external influences. For example I began a Master of Arts after graduating the Con, and while the theory and philosophy gave me a lot of knowledge and growth it also blocked off other paths, including moving to NYC or Europe like a lot of my closest friends. Some of them have had great success - Downbeat Awards and Grammys - and decisions like the Masters pulled me away from being able to share in their experiences.

Another lesson that is close to regret is how long it took me to stop playing for other people, rather than playing for myself. When we play for applause rather than focus on our own unique authentic artistry we do something profoundly flawed. The audience are always a placeholder for an abstract ideal that is unachievable - we try to make what the Other would want, and we can only judge success by symbols (like applause), its an important point because it weakens our own internal compass of what is good and bad. Good is what is true, and we see that from all the greats, they did something different precisely because they did what they wanted to listen to, or watch, and they therefore knew exactly what they were doing, with authenticity, at every step. It took me way to long to grasp how trying to impress others is deeply corrupting to artistry.

# What was ur fondest memory from playing and touring?

Well some of the European tours seem memorable - we did some big gigs in amazing places. But when I think of those experiences they were kind of painful. Long hours on tour buses, constantly moving, having to share space with the same people for weeks on end. A lot of musicians will say the same. 

Possibly my fondest memories were seeing others play. I saw shows in NYC that had me walking the streets in rapture, when you're deep into your art the level of inspiration you can get from others is really elevated, so I cite this as a fond memory.

OK I've got a good one, a great experience. In my final years in WAYJO we had a legendary musical director. We toured to the Manly Jazz Festial and after our first show this guy figured out my roommate and I had bought vodka. He waited until we would be a couple of glasses in then had the assistant call our room and ask us to be in the hotel lobby in 10 minutes with our horns. He took us in a taxi to a jazz show by another legendary Australian jazz musician, this was a festival show, and he'd arranged for us to get on stage and play with the guy. When we began playing the older musicians deliberately shifted keys and time signatures to make it tough for us - the whole thing was a set up, partly to inspire us but mostly to discourage drinking on tour. In hindsight the experience was absolutely terrifying but incredibly motivating. The old jazz guys were part teaching us how to live, part making a big practical joke at our expense. Absolutely cherish that memory. 

Equal top would be getting a scholarship to the one-time-only week-long jazz program at the National Academy of Music (at the time it was based in the old South Melbourne town hall). It was 25 musicians all under 25 years old, from around the country, in one place for a week to work together. Amazing fun, great experience, it ended with us performing at the Wangaratta Jazz Festival and the famed Bennetts Lane jazz club.
